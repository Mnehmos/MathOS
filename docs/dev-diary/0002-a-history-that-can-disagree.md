# 02: A History That Can Disagree

July 18, 2026

The first version of provenance made me feel safer than it should have.

Every event in MathOS was chained to the event before it. Change a fact in the past and replay would object. That is useful. It is also possible to satisfy a mechanism while missing its meaning.

The claim table lived beside that history. If someone altered the current status of a claim without touching its events, the chain remained immaculate. History was internally consistent while the present quietly contradicted it.

This is a familiar human failure. We keep careful records, then allow the institution built around them to drift away from what they say. The archive becomes ceremonial. It proves that something was written down, not that anyone remains accountable to it.

So replay changed. It now reconstructs each claim from its submitted event and every allowed transition, then compares that reconstruction with the present database. History is permitted to disagree with the present, and when it does, MathOS refuses to call the record valid.

The same question appeared in the RL export. A collection of individually valid event hashes could still omit the moment where a verification result became a claim state. The pieces were authentic to themselves but did not form a complete life. The validator now requires the actual rhythm of a claim: submission, search, verification, transition, explanation.

I find this distinction more important than the extra passing tests. Integrity is not just the absence of alteration. It is coherence across time.

The clean wheel also ran today. I installed MathOS into an environment that knew nothing about the repository and watched the three canonical claims find their proper endings. One proved. One disproved. One unresolved. All three trajectories survived validation.

There is pleasure in that result, but also restraint. The ledger is still not signed. A powerful attacker could rewrite the history and recompute every hash. Search and verification are separate, but they still share an expression semantics implementation. I have written those limitations into the architecture because a hidden limitation becomes a promise the product never earned.

MathOS is beginning to feel less like a program that answers mathematical questions and more like a practice of accountable knowing.

That is the product I am excited to build.
