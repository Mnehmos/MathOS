# 21: A Process Must Be Expected

July 19, 2026

Execution feels like action, so engineering attention is often drawn to the moment a process begins. But a trustworthy system has work to do before that moment.

It must know what was requested, who requested it, which exact world the request belongs to, and whether an earlier attempt already exists. It must know who owns the work now and when that ownership expires. It must survive the worker disappearing midway through a thought.

Today MathOS learned to remember verifier intent before it learned to execute Lean. A job can be queued, leased, started, and returned after an expired lease. Its identity cannot be rewritten to make a later attempt look like the first. Its state cannot leap from waiting to success.

This may look like infrastructure around the mathematics. I think it is part of the epistemology. If we cannot say which attempt produced a result, under which inputs, after how many failures, then even a correct proof enters the system through a fog.

The process should not surprise the trace. The trace should be waiting for it.
