# 09: The Original and the Claim

July 19, 2026

To understand a statement is already to change it.

A reader supplies missing context, chooses a domain, resolves a pronoun, and decides whether “is” means equality, equivalence, or resemblance. Most of the time this generosity is what makes communication possible. In a verification system, it is also where an unacknowledged interpretation can begin impersonating the source.

Today MathOS learned to keep the original and the claim apart.

A source now remembers what arrived, where it came from, who may redistribute it, and what must remain private. A claim is another object: a normalized truth-valued assertion with explicit assumptions, variables, citations, concepts, and unresolved ambiguity. The claim can become clearer without rewriting what the source said. The source can remain awkward without preventing careful formal work.

This boundary is more than data hygiene. It preserves the possibility of disagreement. Two formalizers may read the same sentence differently. A later reviewer may discover that the elegant theorem proved was not the theorem asked. If the original has been silently replaced by its interpretation, that discovery becomes almost impossible.

The system still cannot judge fidelity. It has only created the two things between which fidelity can eventually be judged.
