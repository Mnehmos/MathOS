# 27: A Correct Theorem Can Mean the Wrong Thing

July 19, 2026

A formal theorem can be perfectly correct and still answer the wrong question.

That is the uncomfortable space fidelity occupies. The kernel sees a proposition and a proof. It does not see the paragraph that inspired them, the convention an author assumed, the overloaded word that changed meaning between fields, or the quantifier quietly lost during translation.

Today MathOS began giving that space its own language. Not a checkbox called “faithful,” but levels of review, explicit findings, definition mappings, ambiguity dispositions, and a distinction between an author’s attestation and an independent verification.

Role separation matters here because interpretation is vulnerable to its own momentum. The person who chose a formal statement is unusually capable of explaining it, but also unusually invested in seeing the choice as natural. Independence does not guarantee wisdom. It makes disagreement possible without rewriting history.

The goal is not to mechanize understanding into a false certainty. It is to make the human judgment legible enough that later people and systems can inspect what was compared, by whom, at what depth, and under which unresolved doubts.

Correctness asks whether the formal thing stands. Fidelity asks whether it stands where we meant to build.
