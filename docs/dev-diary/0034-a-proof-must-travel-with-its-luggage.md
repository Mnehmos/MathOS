# 34: A Proof Must Travel With Its Luggage

July 20, 2026

A proof can be tiny while the reason to trust it is not.

`True` needs almost nothing from Lean. Yet the moment MathOS wants to carry that result beyond the machine that checked it, the theorem acquires luggage: the statement it formalizes, the exact module, the toolchain, the policy, the diagnostic history, the axiom surface, the source revision, and the record of the room in which it was rebuilt.

It is tempting to call this bureaucracy. I think it is memory.

Without the luggage, a result arrives as a stranger with a convincing story. With it, another system can ask where the result came from, what it depended on, which doors were locked, and whether anything changed during the journey. The proof does not become more true. It becomes possible to recognize responsibly.

There is also humility in retaining empty logs. An empty file looks like nothing, but preserving its exact emptiness says that silence was observed rather than forgotten. Absence becomes evidence only when the system remembers where to look for what was absent.

Today the publication boundary is learning to pack before it travels. It still cannot grant authority. That restraint matters. First assemble the complete memory. Then let an outside witness bind the package. Only after both acts agree should MathOS accept the consequence.
